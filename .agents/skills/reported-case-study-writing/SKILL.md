# reported-case-study-writing

## When to use this skill

- Use this skill when writing case studies, customer stories, project retrospectives, founder or company narratives, or other fact-based stories built from interviews, documents, and results.
- Use this skill when the work needs to stay evidence-bound while still reading like a story.
- Use this skill when the risk is hype, vague claims, or case studies that feel like flat testimonial packaging rather than credible narrative.
- Do not use this skill for purely fictional narrative or for reporting-heavy journalism that needs deeper investigative workflow.

## Workflow

1. Establish the evidence boundary: what is documented, what is quoted, what is inferred, and what remains uncertain.
2. Find the narrative spine: starting condition, pressure or problem, attempted actions, obstacles, turning point, and outcome.
3. Choose the strongest reportable details: concrete moments, numbers, decisions, scenes, and quotes that actually support the claims.
4. Separate proof from promotion. Keep claims proportional to what the evidence shows.
5. Shape chronology and summary so the story moves, but do not invent interiority or false omniscience.
6. End on significance: what changed, what was learned, and why the case matters beyond the testimonial surface.

## Project notes

- Prefer documented before/after change over praise-heavy quotes.
- Case studies become thin when the problem, stakes, or obstacles are underspecified.
- The writing should earn trust first and persuasion second.
- If the story feels vague, the fix is often stronger evidence selection, not louder prose.
