# narrative-nonfiction

## When to use this skill

- Use this skill when the work must remain tied to real events, reporting, documents, interviews, or lived experience.
- Use this skill for essays, memoir, profiles, case studies, reported features, founder narratives, and explanatory storytelling grounded in facts.
- Use this skill when the challenge is to create scenes, movement, and meaning without inventing unsupported interiority or overclaiming what the evidence can bear.
- Do not use this skill for fully invented fiction or for copy-only persuasion work detached from evidence.

## Workflow

1. Establish the factual boundary first: what is known directly, what is inferred, and what remains uncertain.
2. Identify the narrative spine: situation, pressure, sequence, and the human stakes the material actually supports.
3. Decide what deserves scene versus summary. Use reported scenes where evidence is strong enough; compress or qualify where it is not.
4. Shape chronology and structure intentionally. Reorder only when it clarifies rather than distorts.
5. Preserve voice and meaning without faking intimacy. Let significance emerge from reported pattern, consequence, and discovery.
6. Revise for honesty and force together: remove vague filler, unsupported certainty, false drama, and explanation that the material has not earned.

## Project notes

- Reported storytelling is strongest when structure answers to evidence.
- Do not write as though access was deeper than the reporting allows.
- Prefer concrete observed or documented detail over generalized “content” language.
- If the draft feels thin, the fix may be better reporting or better selection, not louder prose.
