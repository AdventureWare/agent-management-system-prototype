# voice-and-narration

## When to use this skill

- Use this skill when narrative writing sounds generic, anonymous, tonally confused, or detached from a real telling presence.
- Use this skill when the main problem is narrator stance, point of view, distance, voice, or prose that does not feel shaped by perception.
- Use this skill when AI-generated narrative is grammatically fine but still sounds like it could have been written by anyone.
- Do not use this skill when the main issue is reporting gaps, scene structure, or character desire without a significant narration problem.

## Workflow

1. Identify who is telling, through what perspective, and at what distance from the material.
2. Decide what kind of linguistic presence the draft needs: intimate, restrained, ironic, reflective, reportorial, lyrical, or otherwise.
3. Check whether diction, syntax, rhythm, and selected detail actually reflect that narrator or perspective.
4. Remove generic competent phrasing and replace it with more particular perception, emphasis, and sentence movement.
5. Re-check consistency across the whole draft so the narration stays intentional without becoming monotonous.
6. If needed, adjust scene presentation, description, and exposition so voice is carried by the prose itself rather than by surface quirks alone.

## Project notes

- Voice is not ornament. It is a function of stance, perception, syntax, and selection.
- Point of view and narrator are related but not identical; do not collapse them lazily.
- If a draft feels “AI,” the likely problem is often weak narrator stance or weak detail selection rather than weak grammar.
