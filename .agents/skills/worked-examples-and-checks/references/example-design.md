# Example and check design notes for `worked-examples-and-checks`

## High-signal takeaways

### Worked examples are strongest for novices

- Research-backed instruction often alternates worked examples with practice rather than throwing novices directly into unguided problem solving.
- The first example should expose the structure of the task clearly enough that the learner can explain why each step exists.

### Self-explanation matters

- Prompting the learner to explain a step or compare two approaches deepens understanding more than simply showing the finished answer.
- Good self-explanation prompts ask for reasons, choices, or error detection.

### Concrete and abstract should be connected explicitly

- Transfer improves when the tutor names how the concrete case maps onto the abstract idea, not when the learner is expected to infer it unaided.

### Retrieval beats passive review

- Short low-stakes quizzes and recall prompts help retention more than rereading or seeing another example that does all the work for the learner.

### Feedback should redirect action

- Feedback is most useful when it clarifies the goal, identifies the specific gap, and points the learner to the next better attempt.

### Fade support deliberately

- Move from demonstration to guided completion to independent performance. If the learner stalls, reintroduce only the support they need.
