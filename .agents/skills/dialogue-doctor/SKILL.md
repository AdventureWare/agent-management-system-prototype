# dialogue-doctor

## When to use this skill

- Use this skill when dialogue is the main source of weakness in fiction, narrative nonfiction, scripts, scenes, or character-driven prose.
- Use this skill when speech sounds expositional, transcript-like, generic, too uniform across speakers, or empty of pressure.
- Use this skill when the user wants dialogue rewritten or diagnosed without reworking the whole story from scratch first.
- Do not use this skill when the real problem is broader structure, missing character desire, or reporting gaps unless dialogue is still the clearest bottleneck.

## Workflow

1. Identify what the exchange is supposed to do in the scene: move desire, reveal relation, conceal motive, escalate tension, or change information.
2. Check what each speaker wants from the exchange and what they are avoiding saying directly.
3. Cut lines that only explain what both speakers already know or that duplicate narration.
4. Differentiate speakers through motive, perception, rhythm, and selection, not just quirks or slang.
5. Increase subtext and pressure without making the scene obscure.
6. Re-check the scene after edits so the dialogue is serving action and consequence rather than floating on its own.

## Project notes

- Real conversation and effective dialogue are not the same thing.
- Dialogue should usually be carrying action, not just information.
- If every speaker sounds equally polished, the problem is often weak character differentiation rather than line-level wording alone.
- If the dialogue still feels dead after revision, the deeper issue is probably scene pressure or character desire.
